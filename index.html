Overview

Exoplanets are planets orbiting stars other than the Sun. Since the first confirmed detections in the 1990s, astronomers have confirmed over 5,000 exoplanets using many methods. Studying them tells us how planets form, how common Earth-like worlds might be, and where to search for possible life.

Quick summary

  • Detection: Transit, radial velocity, direct imaging, microlensing.
  • Types: Hot Jupiters, super-Earths, mini-Neptunes, rocky Earth-like planets.
  • Missions: Kepler, TESS, JWST, Roman Telescope (upcoming).

Why exoplanets are important

Exoplanets reveal the full range of planetary systems ‚Äî many different from our Solar System. They let us test planet formation theories and search for atmospheric signs (biosignatures) that could hint at life.

Detection Methods ‚Äî In detail

Transit Method

The transit method detects a periodic dip in a star's brightness when a planet crosses (transits) the star from our viewpoint. By measuring the dip depth and duration we can calculate the planet's radius and orbital period. Kepler and TESS use this method.

Radial Velocity (Doppler)

Radial velocity measures shifts in a star's spectrum as it moves toward or away from us due to a planet's gravitational pull. This gives a minimum mass for the planet and, combined with transit radius, yields density.

Direct Imaging

Direct imaging blocks out starlight (coronagraphs / starshades) to photograph massive, young, widely separated planets.

Gravitational Microlensing

Microlensing uses a foreground star as a lens; if a planet orbits that star it creates a short-duration blip in the lensing light curve. Microlensing can detect planets far from their stars and even free-floating (rogue) planets.

Types of Exoplanets

Hot Jupiters

Gas giants with orbital periods of days ‚Äî orbiting very close to their stars. They taught astronomers about planet migration.

Super-Earths & Mini-Neptunes

Planets with radii between Earth and Neptune. Many are volatile-rich or possess thick atmospheres ‚Äî a common class in the galaxy.

Earth-like Rocky Worlds

Smaller, rocky planets. Those in the habitable zone (where liquid water is possible) are prime targets for biosignature searches.

Rogue Planets

Planets not gravitationally bound to any star, detected via microlensing.

Habitable zone note: that zone depends on the star's luminosity and the planet's atmosphere ‚Äî distance alone does not guarantee habitability.

Key Missions & Observatories

Kepler

Launched 2009. Targeted a single patch of sky to measure transits and determine planet occurrence rates.

TESS

Transiting Exoplanet Survey Satellite ‚Äî an all-sky survey focused on bright nearby stars for follow-up by ground telescopes.

JWST

James Webb Space Telescope provides high-resolution infrared spectra ‚Äî ideal for measuring exoplanet atmospheres and molecules like H2O, CO2 and CH4.

Roman Telescope (upcoming)

NASA's Nancy Grace Roman Space Telescope (launch mid-2020s) will contribute wide-field surveys and microlensing searches for planets.

Atmospheric Studies & Biosignatures

Spectroscopy during transits reveals atmospheric absorption features. Key molecules of interest include:

  • Water vapour (H2O) ‚Äî common in warm atmospheres.
  • Methane (CH4) ‚Äî can be produced biologically or geologically.
  • Oxygen & Ozone (O2, O3) ‚Äî potential biosignatures but also produced abiotically.
  • Carbon dioxide (CO2) ‚Äî important greenhouse gas; influences surface temperature.

Interpreting biosignatures requires context: planet temperature, stellar activity, and atmospheric chemistry all matter.

Fun Facts

  • There are more known exoplanets now than the total planets in our Solar System.
  • Hot Jupiters can orbit their star in just a few days.
  • Some planets orbit two stars (circumbinary planets) like Tatooine in Star Wars.
  • The TRAPPIST-1 system has at least 7 Earth-sized planets.
